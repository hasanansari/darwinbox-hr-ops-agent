# Darwinbox HR Operations Policy Handbook

*Effective for all full-time employees globally. This document is the canonical source for the
numeric thresholds referenced elsewhere in the HR Ops platform (anomaly detection, compliance
rules engine) — those systems were built to enforce exactly the limits stated here, not the other
way around.*

## 1. Annual Leave and Quarterly Limits

Every full-time employee accrues **24 days of annual leave per year**, credited at the start of the
calendar year. Leave does not roll over between years except where required by local law.

To prevent both under- and over-use of leave, each employee's leave-taking is reviewed against a
**quarterly soft limit of 15 days**. Taking more than 15 days of leave within a single quarter does
not require denial, but it triggers a review: the employee's manager is notified and asked to confirm
the leave pattern is legitimate (e.g. a planned vacation, a medical situation) rather than a sign of
disengagement or a scheduling abuse pattern.

A specific pattern HR Ops watches for is leave **clustered around long weekends** — repeatedly taking
single days off on Mondays or Fridays to extend a weekend. This is not prohibited outright, but when
more than half of an employee's leave in a quarter follows this pattern, it is flagged for manager
review regardless of the total number of days taken.

## 2. Maternity, Paternity, and Medical Leave

Maternity leave is **26 weeks** of fully paid leave, available to any employee who has completed at
least 80 days of continuous employment prior to the expected date of delivery. Paternity leave is
**2 weeks** of fully paid leave, available within 3 months of the child's birth or adoption.
Medical leave for a documented illness or injury is granted in addition to annual leave and does not
count against the quarterly 15-day limit described in Section 1.

## 3. Overtime Policy

Overtime is intended to be the exception, not a routine extension of the work week. Employees should
not regularly exceed **12 hours of overtime per week**. Overtime up to roughly double that
threshold (around 24 hours/week) is treated as a moderate breach requiring HR visibility but not
escalation; sustained or repeated overtime meaningfully above that level is treated as a serious
compliance concern requiring direct HR intervention, since it may indicate understaffing,
unsustainable workload, or — in some jurisdictions — a legal exposure around mandated rest periods.

## 4. Payroll Correction and Approval Tiers

Payroll discrepancies are corrected through a tiered approval process based on the size of the
discrepancy, to balance speed against the risk of an unreviewed mistake:

- **Under $2,000**: may be corrected directly once verified against the payroll system.
- **$2,000–$5,000**: requires the employee's manager to review and approve the correction before
  it is applied.
- **$5,000 or more**: requires HR sign-off in addition to manager review, regardless of confidence
  in the underlying calculation. Large corrections are never auto-applied.

Payroll outliers identified within the Finance department itself always require HR sign-off,
regardless of size — Finance also administers payroll, and this is a basic segregation-of-duties
safeguard against even the appearance of self-correction.

## 5. Probationary Period

New employees are considered to be in a **probationary period for their first 3 months (90 days)**
of employment. During probation, any payroll, leave, or compliance issue affecting that employee is
escalated to their manager for direct review rather than being corrected automatically — statistical
or automated judgments are considered less reliable for an employee whose baseline behavior and pay
records are still being established.

## 6. Mandatory Training and Compliance

All employees must complete mandatory compliance training (covering workplace conduct, data
handling, and safety) within their first 90 days, and on an annual refresher cadence thereafter.
A missing training record cannot be resolved automatically — software cannot complete a training
course on an employee's behalf — and is always escalated to the employee's manager, who is
responsible for ensuring their reports remain current on required training.

## 7. Performance and Attendance Expectations

Employees are expected to maintain reasonable attendance, generally above 90% of scheduled working
days, barring approved leave. When an attendance shortfall coincides with an unusual leave pattern
(see Section 1), HR Ops treats this as a signal that the underlying issue may be broader than leave
policy alone, and routes it for manager review rather than handling it as a routine leave matter.
Employees on a documented low performance rating receive the same heightened review treatment for
payroll and leave anomalies, since automated correction without management visibility is
inappropriate when a broader performance conversation may already be underway.
